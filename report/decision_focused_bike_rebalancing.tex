\section{Introduction}

In the last five years, the U.S. has continued to invest in docked bike sharing systems, growing total docks by 40\% to a total of almost 10,000 \cite{bts2026bikeshare}. As these systems continue to scale, efficient
operations remains a critical challenge.

In docked bike sharing systems, stations with specific parking spots, or docks, are installed for users to rent and return bikes. Importantly, each station has fixed capacity and must balance both the availability of bikes 
for outgoing rentals and the availability of docks for incoming returns. As such, operators consistently engage in rebalancing, where bikes are strategically moved between stations. Nonetheless, determining exactly how a system should be rebalanced
is difficult and a rich area of research.

Central to bike rebalancing is the determination of each station's optimal, or target, level of inventory. Traditionally, this is done by using historical data to build a predictive model 
of each station's demand. Then, these predictions are used as an input to an optimization model to jointly solve for the stations' target inventories. While modern approaches 
have started leveraging advances in machine learning for better predictive modeling \cite{zhou2022pto}, many continue to rely on the Predict-then-Optimize (PTO) approach, treating prediction and optimization tasks independently. 

However, recent work \cite{amos2017optnet, elmachtoub2022spo} has enabled predictive models to be trained in-the-loop with downstream optimization tasks. This new approach, Decision-Focused-Learning (DFL), has shown
promising results by better aligning machine learning models with downstream utility instead of upstream accuracy \cite{donti2017task}. 

In this work, we explore the performance of the DFL approach in the context of bike rebalancing. Specifically, we solve for stations' target inventories by predicting the net demand at each station and solving a mixed integer program (MIP).
We make use of the SPO+ surrogate loss to allow our predictive model to be trained end-to-end. We explore our approach's performance on historical bike sharing
data from Pittsburgh (POGOH and Healthy Ride). These datasets are particularly interesting, since Pittsburgh's distinct seasonality and topology create meaningful asymmetries in demand patterns \cite{kim2020pgh}.

In short, our work finds that decision loss is outperformed by traditional mean squared error (MSE) in three of four experiments and is unable to achieve meaningful improvements in bike rebalancing. 

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Rebalancing in Bike Sharing Systems.}
Bike rebalancing has been widely studied and generally consists of solving two problems: stations' target inventories and the subsequent vehicle routing when executing rebalancing. These problems are often modeled as 
MIPs \cite{ejor2024rebalancing} and can broadly be classified by when the rebalancing is occurring: static (overnight) or dynamic (intra-day). Given the reduced complexity from assuming overnight rebalancing, many papers focus on the static setting and, until recently,
solved the two problems independently. However, recent work by \citet{schuijbroek2017irvr} has demonstrated the value of solving for inventory targets and routes jointly. Nonetheless, these methods typically implement the PTO approach and treat 
demand forecasts as an independent input to optimization problems. This two-staged approach allows for a potential disconnect between prediction accuracy and actual task performance.

\paragraph{Decision-Focused Learning.}
In order to directly align models with their downstream performance, significant work has been done to enable end-to-end learning. This approach, termed DFL, relies on numerous techniques for differentiating or approximating gradients through optimization problems \cite{mandi2024DFL}.
A popular approach is to use the SPO+ surrogate loss function introduced by \citet{elmachtoub2022spo}, which approximates downstream decision quality and can be used with binary and integer constraints. However, this approach is limited to formulations where the predictions are 
coefficients in a linear objective function. Another popular approach was first proposed by \citet{amos2017optnet}. In this approach, differentiation through the KKT conditions allows for exact gradients. This approach, however, is limited to continuous formulations, though some work 
has demonstrated techniques for application in combinatorial settings \cite{wilder2019melding}.  

\paragraph{Learning-Based Bike Rebalancing.}
Recent work has started exploring applications of machine learning in bike rebalancing. Most natural are efforts to improve demand forecasting through more sophisticated modeling \cite{hulot2018forecasting}. However, many have also started exploring reinforcement learning (RL) for solving 
bike rebalancing \cite{liang2025rl}. While these RL based approaches have shown promising results, they require the development of simulation environments and extensive computation to facilitate learning. Although DFL remains largely unexplored for bike rebalancing, it may offer a good balance of 
leveraging the known structure of the problem (through the optimization model) while still allowing task aware learning.

\section{Methods}
In this work, we implement the simplest bike rebalancing approach, static overnight rebalancing. Specifically, we assume that demand overnight is negligible and that our rebalancing process runs at midnight. Our process starts by predicting the net demand (outflow - inflow) at each station. Then, these net demands are passed to 
a MIP to solve for targets that balance the expected lost demand and over capacity events. To make demand predictions, we rely on cyclical embeddings of time and historical day-ahead weather forecasts. As a baseline, this approach is implemented by training the predictive model independently with MSE. Then, a decision focused version is implemented using the SPO+ loss. We evaluate results by comparing metrics under our static rebalancing assumption and a seperate discrete event-based simulation. 
Details of each component are provided in the following sections.

We utilize historical bike sharing data from the city of Pittsburgh. As shown in \cref{fig:total_rides}, Pittsburgh's bike sharing stations operated as Healthy Ride before being overhauled and renamed POGOH in early 2022. Due to this drastic change,
we treat the two datasets as separate and implement independent experiments for both.

\subsection{Inventory Target Optimization Model}
At the core of most bike rebalancing approaches is an optimization model. In our case, we utilize a MIP to minimize the costs of the predicted ending inventory positions subject to current capacity and inventory constraints. Our original formulation is not amenable to DFL, so we reformulate the problem in a way that allows 
the use of the SPO+ surrogate loss function.

\subsubsection{Original Formulation}
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be the set of stations and $C_i, I_i, \hat{d}_i$ be the capacity, current inventory, and estimated net demand for station $i$, respectively. Additionally, let $c_l$ be the cost of lost demand and $c_o$ the cost of over capacity and $x_{ij}$ designate the amount of bikes to move from station $i$ to $j$. 
Then, our original MIP is:
\begin{align}
\min \quad & \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} (c_l l_i + c_o o_i) \label{eq:orig_objective} \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & I_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ji} - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} + l_i \geq \hat{d}_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S}\label{eq:orig_lost-demand} \\
& I_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ji} - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} - \hat{d}_i - o_i \leq C_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:orig_over_cap} \\
& I_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ji} - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} \leq C_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:orig_start_cap} \\
& \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} \leq I_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:orig_start_inv} \\
& l_i, o_i \geq 0, \quad x_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} && \forall i, j \in \mathcal{S} \nonumber
\end{align}

In this formulation, we use slack variables $l_i$ and $o_i$ to track the amount of lost demand and over capacity that is expected, given our predicted demand and rebalancing decision. Our objective, \cref{eq:orig_objective}, is simply to minimize these total costs. In the constraints, \cref{eq:orig_lost-demand} calculates the lost demand, 
\cref{eq:orig_over_cap} calculates the over capacity, \cref{eq:orig_start_cap} ensures a station is not rebalanced to more than its capacity, and \cref{eq:orig_start_inv} ensures a station does not send out more bikes than its current inventory.

Unfortunately, this formulation does not allow for straightforward DFL implementation. Since the decision variables are integers, applying differentiable optimization techniques \cite{amos2017optnet} is not possible. Moreover, because the predictions appear in the constraints and not in the objective, using the SPO+ loss function is also not possible \cite{elmachtoub2022spo}. 
Curiously, this formulation has a feasible region that changes based on predictions and current inventories. However, the set of targets we can choose is always fixed between $0$ and a station's capacity. We make use of this fact and re-formulate the target optimization model as described in the following section.

\subsubsection{SPO+ Friendly Formulation}
In the original formulation, \cref{eq:orig_lost-demand} and \cref{eq:orig_over_cap} make applying DFL techniques difficult. However, these constraints only exist to calculate the expected costs from selecting a starting inventory level for a station. Since the feasible starting inventory levels for a station are always between $0$ and its capacity, these costs can be 
pre-computed by enumerating all possible inventory levels. Specifically, let $c_{ij}$ denote the cost of setting station $i$ to inventory level $j$. Then, these costs can be pre-computed as: 
\begin{equation}
  c_{ij} = c_l \max(0, \hat{d}_i - j ) + c_o \max(0, j - \hat{d}_i - C_i) \quad \forall j \in \{0, 1, ..., C_i\} \label{eq:cost_head}
\end{equation} 
This operation, which we refer to as a cost head, can simply be repeated for each station to build a matrix of costs. Note, in practice all stations do not have the same capacity, so infeasible targets require masking to store in a matrix. After computing these costs, let $y_{ij}$ denote a binary decision variable for setting station $i$ to level $j$. Then, the target optimization can be re-written 
as an integer program (IP):
\begin{align}
\min \quad & \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \sum\limits_{j=0}^{C_i} (c_{ij} y_{ij}) \label{eq:new_objective} \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & \sum\limits_{j=0}^{C_i} y_{ij} = 1 && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:new_one_target} \\
& \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \sum\limits_{j=0}^{C_i} ~j~y_{ij}  = \mathcal{I} \label{eq:new_total_inv} \\
& x_{ij} \in \{0, 1\} \quad \forall i \in \mathcal{S}, \forall j \in \{0, 1, ..., C_i\} \nonumber
\end{align}
where  $\mathcal{I} = \text{ total inventory } = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} I_i$. Notably, this reformulation expands the number of variables from $| \mathcal{S} |$ to $|\mathcal{S}| (\max{C_i} + 1)$. However, in the case of our Pittsburgh bike sharing systems, this expansion is still a manageable few thousands variables.

Importantly, this reformulation has several, nice properties. First, the computation of the cost inputs in \cref{eq:cost_head} can be done with ReLUs, maintaining the differentiability of the predictive model. Secondly, the feasible region of this reformulation is constant and only depends on the total inventory in the system and 
the capacities of the stations. Finally, the objective is now a linear function of our predicted costs, enabling the use of SPO+ loss function. Using this reformulation, our end-to-end approach is visualized in \cref{fig:end_to_end_dfl}.

\subsubsection{SPO+ Routing Formulation}
We did attempt to extend the SPO+ friendly formulation to include vehicle routing. The objective remained the same, but the chosen targets were constrained by a total routing time of 4 hours utilizing the Miller-Tucker-Zemlin formulation. While this extension is theoretically possible, we found it computationally intractable. This is especially true in the DFL approach, where every iteration of training requires solving 
the joint targets and routing model. 

A potential middle-ground may be approximating routing costs as a network flow problem. In fact, the original formulation can easily be extended by using a distance matrix to add a cost of transportation between stations \cref{eq:orig_objective}. Then, the problem can similarly be transformed into a SPO+ friendly formulation where the objective contains a mix of predicted costs from choosing station targets and known costs from distance matrix. 

To link the flow and targets together, we simply can add a constraint similar to \cref{eq:orig_lost-demand} that the current inventory plus the change from flow equals the chosen target. However, this requires that the feasible region continues to depend on current inventory positions, which violates the static feasible region assumption of the SPO+ loss. It is unknown if SPO+ can still achieve good performance when this assumption is violated. Regardless, the focus of this work is remains on the targets formulation.

\subsection{Simulation}
In addition to evaluating the DFL approach under static rebalancing assumptions (the objective value of the optimization model), we also implement a discrete event-based simulation. Since the simulation is dynamic, it is more realistic and provides insights into
the validity of the static rebalancing assumption. 

At a high level, the simulation starts by executing rebalancing at midnight before stepping through historical trip data. As events occur, the simulation enforces stations' inventory constraints and tracks metrics. 
In the case of a historical trip attempting to rent when a station has no inventory, the trip is discarded and marked as lost demand. Similarly, if a historical trip tries to return to a station with no available docks, the trip is re-routed to the nearest 
station and marked as over capacity. If the nearest station also has no capacity, the bike is forced returned, temporarily allowing for a breach in capacity. This forced returning helps ensure the simulation runs quickly and mimics some unfortunate real-world behavior. 
Detailed pseudocode is provided in \cref{alg:sim_details}.

\subsection{Implementation}
Our implementation sources historical bike sharing data from the Western Pennsylvania Regional Data Center (WPRDC), augmented with historical day-ahead weather forecasts from open-meteo. The predictive models are implemented using PyTorch and the optimization models are implemented
with Gurobi. To facilitate end-to-end training, we rely heavily on PyEPO \cite{tang2024pyeop}. Predictive models were trained using the Adam optimizer with ablations performed across learning rates and weight decay. Our implementation in its entirety can be found at
\url{https://github.com/saloper/decision-focused-bike-rebalancing}.

\subsection{Experiments}
\subsubsection{Models}
For each of our bike sharing datasets we compared: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Naive Capacity:} Bikes are rebalanced proportional to each station's capacity. In the case of fractional bikes, rounding is randomized between stations.
    \item \textbf{Optimal:} Bikes are rebalanced using the approach in \cref{fig:end_to_end_dfl}, but ground truth demand predictions are used. This provides an upper bound on the performance of our static rebalancing approach.
    \item \textbf{Linear MSE Loss:} Demand predictions are trained independently using MSE before executing the approach in \cref{fig:end_to_end_dfl}.
    \item \textbf{Linear SPO+ Loss:} The full implementation of \cref{fig:end_to_end_dfl}, where demand predictions are trained end-to-end using SPO+.
\end{enumerate}

For simplicity, all models were solved with $c_l = 1$ and $c_o = 1$, after some analysis revealed no meaningful change in behavior when these costs are treated unequally. 
\subsubsection{Time Horizons}
Since many bike sharing systems experience morning rushes followed by evening commutes, our daily aggregation of net demand may be inaccurate. 
So, we also completed these experiments focused on solving the morning rush with data only from midnight to noon. This variant will be referred to as the 12hr. 

\section{Results}
\subsection{Metrics}
In all of our experiments we rely on two, key metrics.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean Squared Error (MSE):} The mean squared error between the predicted station demands and the true station demands. This captures the upstream accuracy and is the training criterion in the traditional PTO approach.
    \item \textbf{Cost:} The total cost of lost demand and over capacity events after executing rebalancing. This is calculated both in the objective function of the optimization model (assuming static rebalancing) and by tracking metrics when running the simulation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Performance of Decision Loss for Bike Rebalancing}
The experimental results for both datasets and time-horizons are detailed in \cref{tab:optimization_results}.

First, we observe that the DFL approach fails to improve performance when compared to standard MSE. In fact, MSE outperforms SPO+ in three of the four configurations, with the minimal degradation only on the 24 hour POGOH dataset. 

Second, the large gap between costs estimated in optimization and costs realized in simulation suggests static rebalancing is too strong an assumption, especially for the POGOH dataset. As expected, this gap closes when only estimating morning demand in the 12 hour experiments. 

Finally, the relatively large MSE values, especially in the POGOH dataset, indicate that the predictive models struggle to accurately model demand. In fact, the simple heuristic of spreading the system's inventory proportionally to each station's capacity outperforms all methods, even 
when measured within optimization. Note, the fact that the naive approach also outperforms the optimal in simulation is additional evidence that the static rebalancing assumption is too strong.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{MSE Performance}
It is surprising that even with relevant features, the MSE trained models are unable to achieve good predictive performance. However, analyzing the data revealed two potential causes. A representative station's predictions and ground truth demand are shown in \cref{fig:pred_ex}.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{subfigure}{0.49\linewidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/pred_example_24hr.pdf}
        \caption{Demand over 24 hours cancels out making predictions uninformative.}
        \label{fig:pred_ex_24}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    \begin{subfigure}{0.49\linewidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/pred_example_12hr.pdf}
        \caption{Demand over 12 hours is less symmetric but retains high variance.}
        \label{fig:pred_ex_12}
    \end{subfigure}
    \caption{POGOH Station 10 actual and predicted demand at 24 and 12 hours of aggregation.}
    \label{fig:pred_ex}
\end{figure}

The first challenge, shown in the \cref{fig:pred_ex_24}, is that aggregating to 24 hours results in ground truth demand that is almost pure noise. This is because intraday trips cancel themselves out, resulting in a net demand that is practically mean zero. The challenge, then, is the resulting demand predictions are 
all close to zero, which, when fed to the optimization model, results in many solutions that achieve zero predicted cost. Since the solver picks arbitrarily between these solutions, the resulting targets are almost equivalent to assigning inventory at random.  

Secondly, as shown in \cref{fig:pred_ex_12}, aggregating demand to 12 hours does provide a clearer signal, but there is still significant variance that is difficult to predict with the current features. This results in many predictions that are significantly wrong which directly cause the optimization model to confidently balance inventory to poor targets.


\subsection{SPO+ Performance}
It is also surprising that the SPO+ model is unable to improve upon the performance of the MSE model. In general, decision loss has shown good results in contexts where predictive models are underpowered by "freeing up" models to converge to broad decision boundaries instead of precise predictions. However, 
this effect is not observed in our experiments.   
\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{images/true_demand_boxplots.pdf}
  \caption{POGOH 24 and 12 hour box plots of demand.}
  \label{fig:true_demand_box}
\end{figure}
As shown in \cref{fig:true_demand_box}, one potential cause that is present in both the POGOH and Healthy Ride datasets \cref{fig:true_demand_box_hr} is the lack of clear sink and source nodes. This is especially true when aggregating to 24 hours, where the stations' demands are remarkably similar and centered around zero. When aggregating to 12 hours, 
a few sink and source nodes begin to appear. For example, station 12 experiences large morning in-flows while station 20 experiences large morning outflows. However, even in this case, the vast majority of stations still have relatively balanced demand. The result, then, is SPO+ loss is unable to converge to broad target strategies in the majority of stations. In the stations with
clear sink or source behavior, MSE already captures this by predicting close to the average demand \cref{fig:pred_ex_12}. This effect does not allow SPO+ to improve performance vs. MSE.

\section{Future Work}
While our results show no performance improvements with decision loss, some changes to our approach may help.  
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hybrid Objective:} When demand predictions are small, the optimization model has little information to break ties amongst all the supposed zero cost solutions. So, a small penalty for non-uniformity of inventory targets could be added. The hypothesis is that when demand predictions are low, this penalty would allow the optimization model to choose the zero cost solution with the most even spreading of inventory. 
    \item \textbf{Adding Features:} With the current set of features, baseline prediction performance is poor which makes comparing approaches difficult. Adding features for professional sporting events and school calendars may help, since significant demand in Pittsburgh originates from events and students. Additionally, previous days of demand are correlated, so adding lagged features to make the model autoregressive may also provide some prediction improvements.  
    \item \textbf{Scalability:} The experiments completed are the most simplified version of bike rebalancing: static and targets only. This was because of the significant computation required for implementing DFL approaches. To extend the approach to more practical dynamic and routing models, significant work must be done to improve scalability. One potential idea would be to relax the optimization model to a linear program (LP) for training before solving as a MIP at test time.
\end{itemize}

\section{Simulation Details}
\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{Discrete Event Based Bike Simulation}
\label{alg:sim_details}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Stations $s \in \mathcal{S}$ with capacities $C_s$, Daily event logs $\mathcal{E}_d$
\Require Demand predictor $f_\theta$, Cost head $g_\phi$, Optimization oracle $\mathcal{O}$
\Ensure Cumulative penalties: Lost Demand ($L$), Overcapacity ($O$), Forced Returns ($F$)

\State Initialize $L \gets 0, O \gets 0, F \gets 0$

\For{each day $d$}
    \State \textbf{\% Phase 1: Predict-and-Optimize (Forward Pass)}
    \State Predict demand: $\hat{y} \gets f_\theta(x_d)$
    \State Compute cost coefficients: $c \gets g_\phi(\hat{y})$
    \State Solve for target inventories: $w^* \gets \mathcal{O}(c)$
    \State Rebalance stations: $I_s \gets w^*_s \quad \forall s \in \mathcal{S}$

    \State \textbf{\% Phase 2: Discrete-Event Simulation}
    \For{each event $(t, \text{type}, s)$ in $\mathcal{E}_d$ ordered by time}
        
        \If{\text{type} is \text{rent}}
            \If{$I_s > 0$}
                \State $I_s \gets I_s - 1$
            \Else
                \State $L \gets L + 1$
                \State Mark this specific trip as \textit{failed}
            \EndIf
            
        \ElsIf{\text{type} is \text{return}}
            \If{trip was marked \textit{failed}} 
                \State \textbf{continue} \Comment{Cannot return a bike that was never rented}
            \EndIf
            
            \If{$I_s < C_s$}
                \State $I_s \gets I_s + 1$
            \Else
                \State $O \gets O + 1$ \Comment{Station full; incur penalty}
                \State Let $s'$ be the nearest neighbor to $s$
                \If{$I_{s'} < C_{s'}$}
                    \State $I_{s'} \gets I_{s'} + 1$
                \Else
                    \State $F \gets F + 1$ \Comment{Neighbor also full; force return}
                    \State $I_{s'} \gets I_{s'} + 1$
                \EndIf
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
        
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\State \Return $L, O, F$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
