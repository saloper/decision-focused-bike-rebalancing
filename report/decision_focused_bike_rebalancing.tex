\section{Introduction}

In the last five years, the U.S. has experienced an explosion of docked bike sharing systems, growing total docks by 40\% to almost 10,000 \cite{bts2026bikeshare}. As these systems continue to grow, efficient
operations remains a critical and difficult challenge.

In docked bike sharing systems, stations with specific parking spots, or docks, are created for users to rent and return bikes. Importantly, each station has fixed capacity and must balance both the availability of bikes 
for outgoing rentals and the availability of docks for incoming returns. As such, operators consistently engage in rebalancing, where bikes are strategically moved between stations. Nonetheless, determining exactly how a system should be rebalanced
is difficult and a rich area of research.

Central to bike rebalancing is the determination of each station's optimal, or target, level of inventory. Traditionally, this is done by using historical data to build a predictive model 
of each station's demand. Then, these predictions are used as an input to an optimization model to jointly solve for the stations' target inventories. While modern approaches 
have started leveraging advances in machine learning for better predictive modeling \cite{zhou2022pto}, many continue to rely on the Predict-then-Optimize (PTO) approach, treating prediction and optimization tasks independently. 

However, recent work \cite{amos2017optnet, elmachtoub2022spo} has enabled predictive models to be trained in-the-loop with downstream optimization tasks. This new approach, Decision-Focused-Learning (DFL), has shown
promising results by better aligning machine learning models with downstream utility instead of upstream accuracy \cite{donti2017task}. 

In this work, we explore the performance of the DFL approach in the context of bike rebalancing. Specifically, we solve for stations' target inventories by predicting net demand at each station and solving a mixed integer program (MIP).
Importantly, we make use of the SPO+ surrogate loss to allow our predictive model to be trained end-to-end. We explore our approach's performance on historical bike share
data from Pittsburgh (PoGoh and Healthy Ride). These datasets are particularly interesting, since Pittsburgh's distinct seasonality and topology create meaningful asymmetries in demand patterns.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Rebalancing in Bike Sharing Systems.}
Bike rebalancing has been widely studied and broadly consists of solving two problems: stations' target inventories and the subsequent vehicle routing when executing rebalancing. These problems are often modeled as 
MIPs \cite{ejor2024rebalancing} and can broadly be classified by when the rebalancing is occurring: static (overnight) or dynamic (intra-day). Given the reduced complexity from assuming overnight rebalancing, many papers focus on the static setting and, until recently,
solved the two problems independently. However, recent work by \citet{schuijbroek2017irvr} has demonstrated the value of solving for inventory targets and routes jointly. Nonetheless, these methods typically implement the PTO approach and treat 
demand forecasts as an independent input to optimization problems. This two-staged approach allows for a potential disconnect between prediction accuracy and actual task performance.

\paragraph{Decision-Focused Learning.}
In order to directly align models with their downstream performance, significant work has been done to enable end-to-end learning. This approach, termed DFL, relies on numerous techniques for differentiating or approximating gradients through optimization problems \cite{mandi2024DFL}.
A popular approach is to use the SPO+ surrogate loss function introduced by \citet{elmachtoub2022spo}, which approximates downstream decision quality and can be used with binary and integer constraints. However, this approach is limited to formulations where the predictions are 
coefficients in a linear objective function. Another popular approach was first proposed by \citet{amos2017optnet}. In this approach, differentiation through the KKT conditions allows for exact gradients. This approach, however, is limited to continuous formulations, though some work 
has demonstrated techniques for application in combinatorial settings \cite{wilder2019melding}.  

\paragraph{Learning-Based Bike Rebalancing.}
Recent work has started exploring applications of machine learning in bike rebalancing. Most naturally are efforts to improve demand forecasting through more sophisticated modeling \cite{hulot2018forecasting}. However, many have also started exploring reinforcement learning (RL) for solving 
bike rebalancing \cite{liang2025rl}. While these RL based approaches have shown promising results, they require the development of simulation environments and extensive computation to facilitate learning. Although DFL remains largely unexplored for bike rebalancing, it may offer a good balance of 
leveraging the know structure of the problem (through the optimization model) while still allowing task aware learning.

\section{Methods}
In this work, we assume that demand overnight is negligible and implement the simplest bike rebalancing approach: static overnight rebalancing. Specifically, we assume our model is running at midnight and predict the net demand (outflow - inflow) at each station. Then, these net demands are passed to 
a MIP to solve for targets that balance the expected lost demand and over capacity events. To make demand predictions, we rely on cyclical embeddings of time and historical day-ahead weather forecasts. Importantly, we baseline the results with the standard PTO approach before using the SPO+ loss to implement the 
DFL approach. We evaluate the results with a discrete event based simulation across two historical bike sharing datasets. Details of each component are provided in the following sections.

\subsection{Bike Sharing Dataset}
We utilize historical bike sharing data from the city of Pittsburgh. As shown in \cref{fig:total_rides}, Pittsburgh's bike sharing stations operated as Healthy Ride before being overhauled and renamed POGOH in early 2022. Due to this drastic change,
we treat the two datasets as separate and implement independent experiments for both. Pittsburgh's bike sharing data provides a rich testing ground for algorithmic improvements, as the diversity in elevation and weather results in highly asymmetric demand patterns \cite{kim2020pgh}.

Notably, in late 2018, Healthy Ride more than doubled its stations from 50 to 105. Similarly, in July 2023, POGOH increased its station count from 42 to 60. Since, these changes had significant impacts on the intra-station demand dynamics, we ignore all data present before these periods. Additionally, given 
the strong seasonality, we utilize an entire year for our test sets. So, for the Healthy Ride data we train with 2019-2020 and test on 2021. For POGOH, we train with August 2023-2024 and test on 2025.

\subsection{Inventory Target Optimization Model}
At the core of most bike rebalancing approaches is an optimization model. In our case, we utilize a MIP to minimize the expected costs of the ending inventory positions subject to current capacity and inventory constraints. Our original formulation is not amenable to DFL, so we reformulate the problem in way that allows 
the use of the SPO+ surrogate loss function. Details are provided in the following sections.

\subsubsection{Original Formulation}
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be the set of stations and $C_i, I_i, \hat{d}_i$ be the capacity, current inventory, and estimated net demand for station $i$, respectively. Additionally, let $c_l$ be the cost of lost demand and $c_o$ the cost of over capacity and $x_{ij}$ designate the amount of bikes to move from station $i$ to $j$. 
Then, our original MIP is:
\begin{align}
\min \quad & \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} (c_l l_i + c_o o_i) \label{eq:orig_objective} \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & I_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ji} - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} + l_i \geq \hat{d}i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S}\label{eq:orig_lost-demand} \\
& I_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ji} - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} - \hat{d}_i - o_i \leq C_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:orig_over_cap} \\
& I_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ji} - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} \leq C_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:orig_start_cap} \\
& \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} x_{ij} \leq I_i && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:orig_start_inv} \\
& l_i, o_i \geq 0, \quad x_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} && \forall i, j \in \mathcal{S} \nonumber
\end{align}

In this formulation, we use slack variables $l_i$ and $o_i$ to track the amount of lost demand and over capacity that is expected, given our predicted demand and rebalancing decision. Our objective, \cref{eq:orig_objective}, is simply to minimize these total costs. In the constraints, \cref{eq:orig_lost-demand} calculates the lost demand, 
\cref{eq:orig_over_cap} calculates the over capacity, \cref{eq:orig_start_cap} ensures a station is not rebalanced to more than its capacity, and \cref{eq:orig_start_inv} ensures a station does not send out more bikes than its current inventory. 

This formulation, though intuitive, does not allow for straightforward DFL implementation. Since the decision variables are integers, applying differentiable optimization techniques \cite{amos2017optnet} is not possible. Moreover, because the predictions appear in the constraints and not in the objective, using the SPO+ loss function is also not possible \cite{elmachtoub2022spo}. 
This formulation also has a feasible region that changes based on predictions and current inventories. However, the set of targets we can choose is always fixed between $0$ and a station's capacity. We make use of this fact and re-formulate the target optimization model as described in the following section.

\subsubsection{SPO+ Friendly Formulation}
In the original formulation, \cref{eq:orig_lost-demand} and \cref{eq:orig_over_cap} make applying DFL techniques difficult. However, these constraints only exist to calculate the expected costs from selecting a starting inventory level for a station. Since the feasible starting inventory levels for a station are always between $0$ and its capacity, these costs can be 
pre-computed by enumerating all possible inventory levels. Specifically, let $c_{ij}$ denote the cost of setting station $i$ to inventory level $j$. Then, these costs can be pre-computed as: 
\begin{equation}
  c_{ij} = c_l \max(0, \hat{d}_i - j ) + c_o \max(0, j - \hat{d}_i - C_i) \quad \forall j \in \{0, 1, ..., C_i\} \label{eq:cost_head}
\end{equation} 
This operation, referred to as a cost head, can simply be repeated for each station to build a matrix of costs. Note, in practice all stations do not have the same capacity, so infeasible targets require masking to store in a matrix. After computing these costs, we let $y_{ij}$ denote a binary decision variable for setting station $i$ to level $j$. Then, the target optimization can be re-written 
as an integer program (IP):
\begin{align}
\min \quad & \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \sum\limits_{j=0}^{C_i} (c_{ij} y_{ij}) \label{eq:new_objective} \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & \sum\limits_{j=0}^{C_i} y_{ij} = 1 && \forall i \in \mathcal{S} \label{eq:new_one_target} \\
& \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \sum\limits_{j=0}^{C_i} ~j~y_{ij}  = \mathcal{I} \label{eq:new_total_inv} \\
& x_{ij} \in \{0, 1\} \quad \forall i \in \mathcal{S}, \forall j \in \{0, 1, ..., C_i\} \nonumber
\end{align}
where  $\mathcal{I} =$total inventory $= \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} I_i$. Notably, this reformulation expands the number of variables from $| \mathcal{S} |$ to $\mathcal{O}\left(\mathcal{S} C \right)$. However, in the case of our Pittsburgh bike sharing systems, this expansion is still a manageable few thousands variables.

Importantly, this reformulation has several, nice properties. First, the computation of the cost inputs in \cref{eq:cost_head} can be done with ReLUs, maintaining the differentiability of the predictive model. Secondly, the feasible region of this reformulation is constant and only depends on the total inventory in the system and 
the capacities of the stations. Finally, the objective is now a linear function of our predicted costs, enabling the use of SPO+ loss function. Using this reformulation, our end-to-end approach is visualized in \cref{fig:end_to_end_dfl}.
